# ToolShare — Strategy Brief

**Project:** A neighborhood tool-sharing app where people list tools they own and borrow from neighbors.

---

## Decision 1: Who Is This For?

**Chosen: The Urban Renter** (Option B)

Urban renters in apartments and condos (ages 25-40) who need tools occasionally but lack the space and budget to own them. This audience has high urgency (real, recurring pain point), lives in dense neighborhoods (short pickup distances), and is already familiar with sharing-economy apps like Uber and Airbnb. The main risk is a supply imbalance — renters are natural borrowers, so the platform will need a targeted strategy to attract lenders (partnering with suburban homeowners or hardware stores for initial supply).

## Decision 2: How Do Neighbors Build Trust?

**Chosen: Neighbor Vouching** (Option C)

New members join via invite from an existing member, who vouches for them. One vouch unlocks basic borrowing; two vouches unlock high-value tools. Over time, reliable members earn the ability to vouch others, creating a trust cascade. This model leverages real social connections (the strongest trust signal), avoids the cold-start problem of reputation systems, and creates a built-in viral growth loop. The tradeoff is slower initial growth, which is acceptable for a neighborhood-scale launch.

## Decision 3: What Should It Look and Feel Like?

**Chosen: Warm Community** (Option A)

Earth tones (#8B7355 accent), rounded corners, soft backgrounds, and a friendly visual language. This direction signals trust and approachability — critical for an app where you're lending physical objects to people in your neighborhood. The warm palette differentiates ToolShare from cold marketplace apps and reinforces the community-first brand. The design should feel like knocking on a neighbor's door, not browsing a catalog.

---

## Summary

ToolShare targets **urban renters** who need tools but can't store them. Trust is built through **neighbor vouching** — real social connections, not anonymous ratings or financial deposits. The visual language is **warm and community-oriented**, using earth tones and rounded forms to make borrowing from a neighbor feel natural and safe. Together, these three decisions create a product that feels less like a marketplace and more like a neighborhood.
